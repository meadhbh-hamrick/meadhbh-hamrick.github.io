Taking Notes

After noting Rat Factor's Note system [1] on Hacker News [2] I realized that I also have a note taking system. Inspired by Mr. Factor's lead, I decided to document it.

Let me start by saying I'm not encouraging you to adopt my system. It works for me, but you're a different person with different requirements. I encourage you to read this page and see if there's anything you can use.

I do encourage you to think about how you take notes. As a sophomore in college I adopted the Cornell System for Note-Taking [3] and it helped greatly. Simple changes in the way you think about note-taking can notably improve the utility of your notes and meeting minutes.

While I keep telling myself I will adapt this system to a note-taking app, it it mostly useful for taking notes on pen and paper. You can use it with virtually any word processor or text editor that supports unicode.

I use either a 4-colour ballpoint pen (like this one from BiC [4]), a PILOT 0.38mm Juice Gel Ink Ballpoint Pen 12 Color set [5], or a PILOT Disposable Fountain Pen 3 Color Set [6]. My system does not require the use of coloured ink and you are under no obligation to use coloured ink in your note-taking system. Props to Mr. Factor (aka Dave Gauer) for recommending the PILOT 0.38mm Juice Gel Ink pen.

Journal Entries

Let me start with Journal Entries since Meeting Minutes are simple extensions of the journal entry system.

The purpose of the journal entry system is to "get ideas out of my head" or "declutter my mind" as David Allen says [7]. The reason I developed this system was to improve my ability to find details in notes I had written to myself. I use a Zettelkasten type system [8] for writing notes about specific subjects, but my journals are a chronological record of random thoughts, important details and random things I need to get out of my head so I can get back to useful work.

Writing notes is easy. Finding notes you've written is hard. My note taking system is intended to help find things you've written down. Each of the "callouts" in this document represents something I've needed to find in my notes, but couldn't. I have callouts for to-do items, open questions, important changes, agreements and random important things. You may want to add your own.

So what does a journal entry look like? I write them down as a header with a line across the page, a date, some hash-tags and a blank space for a QR Code. I haven't started putting QR Codes in my journal, but have a fantasy I'll eventually do it. If I didn't carve out a small space for the QR Code I would go nuts thinking about how I'll obscure the notes when I do eventually put QR Code stickers in my journals.

This is reasonable approximation of what my journal looks like:

2022-02-26 ♀
#Notes, #Think about it

QR Code Goes Here (Someday)

And here's where the text starts.

Just imagine this was filled with "real" text, talking about "real" things.

2022-02-27 ♃
#Notes, #Moar Thinks

QR Code Goes Here (Someday)

Sometimes a page has multiple entries. Who knew?

Hell is other peoples' journals.

A few obvious things: First, it's in black ink. I like black ink. When I was in the marines, the only type of pens we had were black pens. Blue pens were for civilians. If you want to see a Marine admin clerk freak out, use a blue pen on an official form. But old habits die hard and I like black ink for notes so I can use blue, red, green (and sometimes purple) to make callouts easier to find.

Secondly, I use planetary symbols after the dates to denote the day of the week. You should probably just put the first couple of letters of the day. If you want to use planetary symbols, these are the ones I use:

☉ Sunday
☽ Monday
♂ Tuesday
☿ Wednesday
♃ Thursday
♀ Friday
♄ Saturday

Lastly, it's clear I like to indent lines / paragraphs in my journals. When I give an example of callouts below, it will be more obvious why I indent everything: I don't indent callouts, so they're much easier to see.

About To-Do Items

One day I had to find a pair of to-do items in my journal. It turns out they were kind of hard to find. I realized they would be easier to find if I included an "icon" next to them. I originally used a triangle, but later changed to a box. When I completed a task, I went back and put a check-mark in the box. If the task was "overtaken by events" and would not be completed, I put an "X" through it. When I left the marines and could use blue pens again, I sometimes wrote to-do items in blue. My colour vision is good enough that blue stands out against the black of the normal text. That's not true for everyone; feel free to pick a different colour. Or no colour. It's up to you.

After more trial and error, I figured out that padding around to-do items and no indentation makes them easier to spot. Here's an example of a journal page with to-do items:

1990-08-02 ♀
#marines

QR Code Goes Here (Someday)

Wondering if I'm going to get called up out of the IRR.

☑ Call Kansas City and ask if my DD-214 was mailed out.

Maybe I should try to find my gear in case they call me back or I have to prove I still have it.

☑ Get gear out of storage.

☒ Inventory gear. Is it all there?

1990-08-06 ☽
#marines

QR Code Goes Here (Someday)

Called Kansas City, they said the DD-214 was mailed out last Thursday.

From this example, I can see that I listed several to-do items. The first two I completed, but the last one was abandoned, likely because I felt it was unlikely I would be called out of the Individual Ready Reserve after talking with the DoD Finance Office in Kansas City.

When Things Change

Sometimes things change. Important changes get a red exclamation point. Consider this example:

1987-06-01 ☽
#marines #reserve

QR Code Goes Here (Someday)

SgtMaj Havens says there's no way I'm going to A-School this summer; it's okay to register for summer classes.

1987-06-12 ♀
#marines

QR Code Goes Here (Someday)

!  SgtMaj Havens called to say he has orders for me to go to my A-School at LeJeune.

Called registrar, I can drop Psych class as incomplete-passing and finish it next fall.

☑ (1987-06-14) Get a note from MAG-41 to housing office so I can get out of my dorm lease without penalty.

In this example we can see that Sergeant Major Havens had "incomplete information" about my second increment of Initial Active Duty for Training Purposes. That I had to drop my Psych class in the summer of '87 and report to Camp LeJeune for training was an important change, so it got a red exclamation point. We can also see that I had to get a note from my reserve unit to give to the university housing office saying I was being activated.

Another thing to note: when to-do items have a due date, I insert the date between the box and the description.

Random Important Things

Sometimes I realize something is important after writing it down. In this case I put a purple dot in the margin. If I don't have a purple pen on me, I'll put a blue dot in the margin. Here's an example:

2022-01-07 ♀
#car #maintenance

QR Code Goes Here (Someday)

I'm pretty sure I don't need to pull maintenance on the Fiat before my trip to the bay area.

⬤ More likely I'll just call the mechanic at (831) 555-1212 and schedule an oil change while I'm down there.

Seems like I'm about 1000 miles shy of when I need an oil change and I bet they can figure out what air filters I need.

What happened here is I wrote down the phone number for a mechanic a friend recommended and later realized it might be a good idea to be able to find that number later. This isn't a major change or a to-do item, so I just put a purple dot next to it.

Meeting Minutes

I used to use the Cornell Note-Taking System [3] for meeting notes, but eventually determined it wasn't the right tool for the job. After a while, my meeting minutes started looking like my journal entries. I don't care about QR Codes for meeting notes. I'm not entirely sure why.

Meeting minutes still have a date and hash-tags in the header, but also a list of attendees.

Callouts for meeting minutes include "open questions" and "agreements". And I still use to-dos, major changes and purple dots.

"Open Question" callouts are just what you would expect: a question that hasn't been answered. I write them in blue ink and preface them with a big question mark. Like to-do callouts, if there's a due date, I insert it between the question mark and the text of the question.

"General Agreement" callouts are notes that the meeting attendees agreed to something. It is often important to be able to find records of such agreements quickly. I use a green star for agreements. I wanted to use a thumbs up sign, but was told that might be an offensive gesture in some cultures. I originally used shaking hands as the "agreement" icon, but most people who saw it said it looked like a raccoon.

Here's an example of what my meeting minutes might look like:

2022-02-23 ☿
Alice, Bob, Sriram, Meadhbh
#aws #switchover #login service

Bob says we're good for service switch-over

★Agree to cut-over old to new on Thursday, March 3rd.

?  Do we have to get approval from IT?

☐ Find out what ticket category to JIRA items go in? Will IT get upset if we use the wrong category?

Alice described the DNS cut-over technique we'll use

Sriram verified we won't need any code changes.

In the meeting we minuted here, there was general agreement when the login-service cut-over would take place. But there's still an outstanding question as to whether we need to get this plan approved by IT and a to-do item to find out what ticket category IT wants us to use.

Field Notes

In my system, "Field Notes" are anything that gets written in a small 3.7" x 5.6" (small) notebook. For me, that's mostly passwords and shopping lists. I should probably mention "Field Notes" is a trademarked term; I usually use it in a generic sense, but the people who make the Field Notes ® note books would probably prefer I use the registered trademark symbol when talking about their brand.

Dave Gauer (aka Rat Factor) recommends thin Field Notes ® books [1]. I prefer PAPERAGE small notebooks [9]. They're almost as easy to lay-flat as the Field Notes ® brand and have a harder cover and include an elastic band to secure them while they're in your pocket or backpack. Field Notes ® notepads are even easier to lay flat and come in a variety of colours and designs. There is no shame in preferring Field Notes ® to PAPERAGE.

A typical entry for me would look like:

2022-02-25 ♀

- Drop off taxes at the post office

- Meals: Veggie Chili, Tofu Thai Curry

- List:
 - Paper Towels
 - Fresh Thai Basil
 - Green Pepper
 - Zucchini or Japanese Eggplant
 - Firm Tofu
 - Lime Leaves
 - Small Mushrooms
 - TVP
 - Eggs
 - Bread

From this you can probably guess I don't bother with callouts on field notes and I eat mostly veggies. With small notebooks, my notes usually take up the whole page so I often ignore lines and almost never include hash-marks.

The purpose of these small notebooks for me is to record thoughts when I'm out of the house / office and to write things down I know I'll need when I'm out. I have a habit of looking at my small notebooks twice a week and when I'm about to walk out the door. (In the example above, I wrote a note to remind myself to grab my taxes if I'm driving anywhere near a post office.)

I have a different small notebook which contains passwords. This one usually never leaves the house. I scribble usernames and passwords on POST-IT ® page markers [10] and then place them in the small notebook. When I change the password, I take the old page marker out and put in a new one. You may also notice I put a line above capital letters and a line below lower case letters.

A Pen of a Different Colour

Some people will tell you not to make colour semantically important because many people are colour blind. And that's very good advice. But my colour vision is pretty good, and this is a description of my personal note-taking system.

That being said, I've noticed my eye-sight deteriorate over the years and can envision a time in the future where I lose colour perception. So I made sure that everywhere colour is used, there's also a unique "icon". It will be harder to find things if I lose my colour vision, but not impossible.

I use a number of different writing implements. I use felt-tip pens and coloured pencils when designing things. I use fountain pens when writing notes to people. In my journal, I currently use several PILOT Juice Gel Ink pens [5]. But for scribbling in small notebooks, I use a BiC 4 Color pen [4].
